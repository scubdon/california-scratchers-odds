An independent ledger of the California Lottery

The real odds.

California publishes more than most states — for every game, the printed odds of each prize and how many of them are still unclaimed. What it still advertises, though, is the overall odds of winning anything, usually a free ticket or a few dollars back. We take their own numbers and turn them into what you actually want to know: the live odds, today, of winning a prize worth keeping — and of clearing $100, $500, or $1,000.

Under each “Win $X+” are two figures: the live odds today (1 in N), and below it the average you'd spend between wins that size — the ticket price times those odds. So ~$2,400 means a typical $100+ win costs about that much in tickets to reach.

Live-odds color shows how much of your stake a prize tier returns on average — its prize divided by its live odds: green a stronger return maroon weaker.

Every Scratcher is a bet with the odds stacked against you — that's how the lottery funds itself. The point of these charts isn't to find a good bet (there isn't one), but to see how bad each game is, and which are merely terrible rather than ruinous. All of it is worked out from California's own published prize tables, and updates with the data.

Outcomes are generated on the fly at each game's published odds — both the per-prize "1 in N" and California's advertised overall odds of winning anything (the gap between the two is the free ticket the lottery hands out, which you'll see modeled here as a free replay). No money is real. This is a simulation, not a guarantee of any particular result.

How this is calculated

California publishes, for every game, a table of each prize level: the printed odds ("1 in N") and how many of that prize are still unclaimed out of how many were printed. From the printed odds and the total number of each prize, we recover the size of the print run, then use the share of prizes still unclaimed to estimate how many tickets are still out there to buy. The live odds of winning a given prize are then:

live odds = tickets still unsold ÷ that prize still unclaimed

These move as the game sells down: once the big prizes are gone, the long odds get even longer — even though the lottery keeps the game on sale. We show both the live odds and the original printed odds so you can see the difference.

These are estimates. They assume the unsold tickets are spread through the pool the same way the printed pool is — which the lottery does not guarantee — and "unclaimed" prizes may already be sitting in a winner's drawer.
